Stakeholder Map

The single biggest mistake on implementations: over-including everyone in every meeting. Use this map to place people in the right tier — then stick to it.

The rule of three tiers: Decide = attends all key meetings, signs off on decisions (max 3–5 people). Contribute = called in for their workstream, not every meeting. Informed = gets the status update email, not in the room.

Tech ↔ Business Bridge

The #1 cause of project friction isn't technology — it's translation failure. Here's how to bridge the gap between what IT is building and what the business needs to understand.

🔄 Translation Guide — Say This, Not That

→
Instead of: "We're configuring the API endpoint and validating the JSON payload schema."
Say: "We're connecting Salesforce and SAP so orders flow automatically — no manual entry needed."
→
Instead of: "There's a governor limit issue causing SOQL query failures in bulk."
Say: "When we process large batches of orders, the system hits a performance limit. We're fixing it now — no impact to go-live."
→
Instead of: "UAT scripts need to cover the P2P integration edge cases."
Say: "Business testers need to try scenarios where orders have special pricing or split shipments — that's where issues tend to hide."
→
Instead of: "We're in hypercare with elevated P1 SLAs."
Say: "For the first 30 days after go-live, IT is on-call with a 2-hour response time for any critical issues."
→
Instead of: "Cutover requires a maintenance window with system downtime."
Say: "On go-live night, the system will be unavailable for approximately 4 hours. Here's the timeline and who to contact."

🎯 When to Involve Business Stakeholders

✅
Involve business early for: defining requirements, reviewing process flows, prioritizing features, and confirming data accuracy during design. Their input here prevents expensive changes later.
⚠️
Don't pull them into: technical architecture decisions, code reviews, or debugging sessions. Translate the outcome, not the process. "We found and fixed an issue" is enough.
✅
Business owns UAT: They define what "correct" looks like. IT supports, not leads. If business users don't test it, they haven't accepted it.
⚠️
Don't surprise them at go-live: If something changed from the original design, tell them early — even if IT resolved it. Surprises at go-live destroy trust.
✅
Use demos, not documents: A 10-minute demo of the working system does more for business buy-in than a 20-page spec. Schedule demos at end of each build sprint.

🚦 Decision Ownership — Who Decides What

IT decides:
Technical architecture, integration patterns, data model design, development approach, tooling choices, environment strategy.
Business decides:
Process changes, what "correct" data looks like, which features are in-scope, training approach, go-live timing relative to business calendar.
Joint decision:
Scope changes that affect both timeline and business process, UAT pass/fail and go-live approval, cutover date, and post-go-live support model.
Escalate when:
IT and business disagree on scope, a decision has been open more than 5 business days, or the decision affects the go-live date. Don't let it sit.

📧 Status Communication by Audience

Executive / Tier 1:
3 bullets max. Overall status (color), top risk, what they need to do. No technical detail. Send weekly — same day, same time.
Business Stakeholders / Tier 2:
Milestone progress, what's changing for them, what you need from them this week. Keep it outcome-focused, not task-focused.
Informed / Tier 3:
Monthly newsletter-style update. "Here's where we are, here's what's coming." No action required from them. Keeps people from feeling blindsided.
End Users:
Bi-weekly as go-live nears. Focus on: what's changing for you, when, and where to get help. Use business language, not project language.

The 5-day rule: If a decision has been open for more than 5 business days with no resolution, it gets escalated to the steering committee in the next meeting. Add a note in the Escalate column.
